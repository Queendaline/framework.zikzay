Boyle’s law and calculation involved

Boyle’s law states that the volume of a given mass of gas is inversely proportional to its pressure provided the temperature remains constant.

Thus, according to Boyle’s law the pressure of the gas increases as the volume decreases and vice versa. This can be expressed mathematically as follows;

           $V~\alpha ~\frac{1}{P}$

          $V=~\frac{k}{P}$

          VP = K

          P1V1 = P2V2

Where;

  • V = volume of pressure
  • K = mathematical constant
  • V1 = Initial volume
  • V2 = Final volume
  • P1 = Initial Pressure
  • P2 = Final Pressure
Calculation involving Boyle’s Law

How the Kinetic Theory Explains Boyle’s law

The pressure exerted by a gas depends on the number of collisions of the particle per unit time on the walls the container. With constant temperature, the rate of motion of the particles will be constant. If the volume of the container is reduced the particles will be confined with a small space (less distance to move), so the number of collisions on the walls of the container will increase. This will result in an increase of the gas pressure, since pressure is determined by the number of collisions per unit time.

On the other hand, if the volume is increased, rate of collisions will decrease, thus resulting in a decrease in gas pressure. Hence, the volume is inversely proportional to pressure and constant T

Definition of Charles’s Law

Charles’s law states that the volume of a given mass of gas is directly proportional to its kelvin or absolute temperature, provided pressure remains constant. Mathematically, the law can be represented as; V α T

          V = KT or $\frac{V}{T}$ = K

The relationship can be expanded in another form.

          $\frac{{V}_{1}}{{T}_{1}}$ = $\frac{{V}_{2}}{{T}_{2}}$

Where;

  • V1 = Initial volume at temperature T1
  • V2 = Final volume at temperature T2
EVIDENCE TO SUPPORT CHARLE’S LAW
  • Air filled balloon expands when the temperature increase at constant atmospheric pressure, until it bursts.
  • Air enclosed in a conical flask expands as it is being heated until it blows off the stopper.
Kinetic Theory Explanation of Charles’s law

With increase in the temperature, the gas molecules acquire more kinetic energy, move faster and collide more often. Therefore, to maintain the same number of collisions on the walls of the container, the volume of the container must increase, hence at constant pressure, the volume is directly proportional to the absolute temperature and vice versa.

Dalton’s law of Partial Pressure

Dalton’s law of partial pressure states that the total pressure of  a mixture of gases is the sum of the pressure which each individual gas would exert if it were confined in the volume occupied by the mixture.

This is expressed mathematically as PTotal = PA + PB + PC

Where PTotal = total pressure of the mixture and PA, PB, PC are the partial pressure exerted by individual gases A and B and C etc

If a gas is collected over water, it is likely to be saturated with water vapor and the total pressure becomes PTotal = Pgas + Pwater vapor

CLASSIFICATION OF SUBSTANCES INTO PURE AND IMPURE

HOMOGENEOUS AND HETEROGENEOUS

Homogeneous and Heterogeneous Materials

In the study of chemistry, substances are called materials. Materials which are familiar to us are wood, salt, sugar, milk, copper, gold, marble, etc. Scientist called all the known material matter. Matter are of two types; homogeneous and heterogeneous.

A homogeneous material is one which is the same throughout. It is material which is formed of substances of same kind. Example are copper, gold, salt, sugar.

A mixture is said to be homogeneous if its constituents have similar physical properties like color, taste or it appears to be uniform composition. A solution of sugar in water is an example of a homogeneous liquid solution. Though the composition can be varied, the mixture will be uniformly colourless and sweet. Other examples of homogeneous are sweat, table salt, blood.

A heterogeneous material is made up of different materials which can be distinguished either with the naked eye or under microscope. Examples are concrete, wood, granite and milk. Milk appears to be homogeneous but under the microscope, particles of various kind can be seen suspended in water, so milk is heterogeneous material.

Heterogeneous mixture, here the constituents are not distributed uniformly with some parts having different physical properties. Examples are muddywater, soil sample, smoke, sandstorm.

Pure and Impure Substances

A pure substance is one that contains no impurity while an impure substance is regarded as a mixture that contains impurity. Example, when a sample of common salt is mixed with some sand, the salt is regarded as impure. The pressure of solid in the salt has made the impure.

Criteria for Purity

Criteria for determining purity of a substance include sharp boiling point, density, sharp melting point, refractive index.

  • Boiling and Melting Point – The melting point of pure solid is specific and can be determined. Each substance has its own melting point. For e.g; ice melts at 0oC (273K). Also, pure liquid substances have specific boiling point; eg, pure w37ater at 100oC (273K) while pure ethanol boils at 78oC and these can be determined in the laboratory.

Impurity in a substance tends to increase the boiling point of a pure liquid while it lowers the melting point of pure substance. For example, impure water boils at temperature above 100oC and impure ethanol boils at a temperature above 78oC.

Ice melts at 0oC(273K). Also, pure liquid substance have specific boiling points eg, pure water boils at 100oC(373K) while ethanol boils at 78OC and these can be determined in the laboratory.

 

 

SEPARATION TECHNIQUE

Method of Separating Mixtures

A mixture contains two or more constituents which can easily be separated by physical methods. There are various methods of separating mixtures such as filtration, evaporation, sieving, decantation, separating funnel, distillation, chromatography, sublimation, etc.

  1. Sieving - is used to separate solid particles of different sizes.

Application -  Sieving is applied in minning industries such as those involving diamond, gold and garri industries

  1. Decantation – is used to separate insoluble solid from a liquid eg a mixture of sand and water. During decantation the solid will settle at the bottom of the container leaving a clear liquid above.

Apparatus used – beaker and glass rod.

Fig. Simple Decantation

  1. Filtration method – is a method used to separate insoluble solid from a liquid. Here the mixture is poured through a porous material, eg, filter paper. During filtration, the liquid portion passes through the filter paper and is called filtrate while the solid material that remains on the filter paper is called the residue, eg, suspension of chalkdust, a mixture of sand water.

Application – used in breweries and water purification plants.

Fig. Filtration method

  1. Evaporation method – is one used to separate soluble solid from a solution eg, a mixture of salt and water. Evaporation dish is not heated directly to;
  • Prevent the decomposition of the solid.
  • To avoid spattering of the solution
  • To prevent breaking of the evaporation dish.

Application – it is used in salt making industries.

Application used – include tripod stand, wire gauge, evaporation dish and water bath.

Fig. Evaporation techniques

  1. Separation funnel method – is used to separate immiscible liquid, eg, a mixture of petrol and water, oil and water, kerosene and water.

Fig. Separation funnel

The use of separating funnel is based on the difference in the polarities of the liquids. Water is a polar solvent, while most organic solvent are non-polar, hence immiscible with water.

Apparatus used – separating funnel, beaker and tripod stand.

  1. Sublimation method – Sublimation is a process whereby some solids when heated, changes directly to gas without passing the liquid state or phase. Such a solid is said to sublime.

Examples of substances that can sublime are iodine, camphor, ammonium chloride, Sulphur naphthalene etc.

Fig. Sublimation

This method is used to separate a mixture of two or more solids in which one of them sublimate at a temperature below the melting point of the other, eg, a mixture of common salt and ammonium chloride. Sublimate is the pure solid obtained when the vapor of a substance which sublimate solidifies.

  1. Precipitation and Distillation method – Precipitation is the method of separation that is based on the difference in solubility of a solid in two different miscible liquids this method is used to separate or salt which is soluble in one solvent forming a mixture with that solvent, but become insoluble when another liquid which mixes well with the mixture but which does not dissolve the salt is added. The salt will be precipitated from the solution and collected by filtration. For example, iron(ii)tetraoxosulphate(vi) is soluble in water to form a mixture (i.e a solution). When ethanol is added to the solution (ethanol is immiscible with water), the iron (ii) tetraoxosulphate(vi) will be precipitated from the solution as it is insoluble in ethanol. This method is carried out by mixing the two different solutions which react to produce one soluble compound and insoluble substance called the precipitate, eg;

Distillation method – Simple distillation is a method used to separate a liquid (solvent) from a solution. The solution is heated in a flask to vaporize the solvent. The vapor passes along condenser which is cooled by circulating water in its outer jacket. The vapor condensed and collected in a separate vessel is called the distillate

The distillate is the pure solvent obtained from the condensed vapor during distillation. The solute and other impurities are left behind in the distillation flask.

Industry applicable – Distillation is used in gin distillation and water distilleries for the manufacture of gin and water respectively. It is also used in wine industry.

Mixtures that can be separated using distillation method are, salt solution, sea water, river or well water, muddy water.
