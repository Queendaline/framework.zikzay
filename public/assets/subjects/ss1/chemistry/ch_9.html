CHAPTER 9

SOLUBILITY OF SALTS

RULES OF SOLUBILITY OF SALTS

  1. All common salts of sodium, potassium and ammonia are soluble in water.
  2. All trioxonitrate(v) salts are soluble in water.
  3. All common chlorides are soluble except silver chlorides, lead(ii)chloride
  4. All common tetraoxosulphate(vi) salts are soluble except BaSO4, PbSO4.
  5. All trioxocarbonate(iv) salts are insoluble in water except Na2CO3, K2CO­3­, and (NH4­)2CO
  6. All hydroxides are insoluble except NaOH, KOH, NH4OH
  7. All sulphides are insoluble except Na2­S, K2S, (NH4)2
  8. All oxides are insoluble except Na2O, K2O, and to some extent CaO and MgO

DEFINITION OF SOLUBILITY

The solubility of a solute in a solvent at a particular temperature is the maximum amount of the solute in moles or grams that will saturate 1dm3 of the Solvent at that temperature.

DETERMINATION OF SOLUBILITY OF SALTS

The determination of the solubility of a given solute in a solvent involves.

  1. Preparing a saturated solution of the given solute at the specified temperature.
  2. Taking a known mass of the saturated solution.
  3. Heating to dryness the known mass of the saturated solution, so that the exact mass of the solvent and the solute, in the saturated solution at that temperature can be calculated.

In mercury, the saturated solution, when the solution is saturated, it is said to be at the state of equilibrium or balance in which the rate of dissolution is equal to the rate of reforming the solid solute, this state of equilibrium is very delicate.

FACTORS AFFECTING (DISRUPTING) THE STATE OF EQUILIBRIUM

  1. Addition of more solute: this result in the production of more molecules or ions until a new equilibrium is attained.
  2. Temperature: the solubility of most solid solute in a solvent increase with increase in temperature.

DEDUCTIONS FROM THE SOLUBILITY CURVES

  1. The solubility of Ca(OH)2 and NaCl are fairly dependent of the temperature.
  2. Between 0oC and 100oC, Ca(OH)2 has the lowest solubility of the six compounds at over 70oC, it shows a decrease in solubility.
  3. The solubility of KCl increases at a constant rate with increase in temperature.
  4. Solubility of KCO­­3 and KNO3 increases very rapidly with increase in temperature.
  5. Na2SO4 shows a solubility curve with a sharp break at 36oC because the salt exist in solution as Na2SO4.10H2O at temperature below 36oC but give up its water of crystallization to become anhydrous (solid) at temperature above 36o This 36oC is referred to as the TRANSITION POINT.

 

IMPORTANCE/APPLICATION/USES OF SOLUBILITY CURVES

  1. A solubility curve helps a chemist to have a quick day reading of solubilities of a substance at any required temperature.
  2. It enables a pharmacist to determine the amount of solid that must be dissolved in a given quantity of solvent to give a prescribed drug mixture.
  3. With the aid of curves, a given quantity of solute can be separated or purified by fractional crystallization.
  4. It enable chemists and research workers to determine the most suitable solvent to be used at various temperature for the extraction of essential chemicals from various natural sources.
  5. It enables us to determine the mass of solute that will crystalize out when saturated solution at higher temperature is cooled at a lower temperature.

 

CALCULATION INVOLVING SOLUBILITY

NOTE: - Number of moles = $\frac{Mass}{Molar~mass}~$

       $\frac{Mass~of~salt}{Molar~mass}\times\frac{1000~of~solvent}{volume~of~solvent}$

Example I: - 3.06g of KClO3 is required to make a saturated solution with 10cm3 of water at 25oC, Calculate the solubility of the salt at 25oC (K=39, Cl=35.5, O=16)

Molar mass of KClO3 = 39 + 35 – 54(16x3) = 122.5gmol-1

No of moles of KClO3 = $\frac{Mass}{Molar~mass}$ = $\frac{3.06}{122.5}$ = 0.025moles

10cm3 of water at 25oC dissolved $\frac{0.025}{10}$ x $\frac{1000}{1}$

                                                = 2.5mol/dm3

OR

$\frac{Mass~of~salt}{Molar~mass}\times\frac{1000~of~solvent}{volume~of~solvent}$

= $\frac{3.06}{122.5}\times\frac{1000}{10}$ = 2.5mol/dm3

Example II: - The solubility of Pb(NO3)2 is 1500g per 1000g of water at 85oC and 500g per 1000g of water at 40oC. Calculate the mass of Pb(NO3)2 that will crystalize out of solution if 120g of the saturated solution at 85oC is cooled to 40oC.

Solution

          Solvent + Solute = Solution

Mass Involved at 85oC = 1000g + 1500g = 2500g

Mass Involved at 40oC = 1000g + 500g = 1500g

Mass of solute deposited on cooling from 85oC to 40oC

          = 2500 – 1500 = 1000g

On cooling from 85oC to 40oC, 2500g of saturated solution deposited 1000g Soluble of  120g of saturated solution will deposit $\frac{1000}{2500}~\times~\frac{120}{1}$ = 48.0g of solute

Example III: - 500cm3 of saturated solution of AlCl3 contains 26.7g in it at 45oC, what is the solubility in moles dm-3 at this temperature (Al=27, Cl=35.5).

Solution

Molar mass of AlCl3­ = 27 + (35.5x3) = 133.5gmol­-1

No of mole of AgCl3 = $\frac{mass}{molar~mass}~$= $\frac{26.7}{133.5}$ = 0.2mole

500cm3 of the solution is saturated by 0.2mole of AlCl3  1000cm3 of the solution will be saturated by $\frac{0.2}{500}~\times~\frac{1000}{1}$ = 0.4 moldm-3
